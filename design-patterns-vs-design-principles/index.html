Design Patterns vs Design Principles

INTRODUCTION

Design patterns and design principles are two important concepts to help us write better code in software development. Two concepts are often considered to be interchangeable terms in the field of software development. However, these two concepts serve different purposes, have distinct characteristics, and are used at different levels of the design process. The explanations below highlight the difference between design patterns and design principles.

DESIGN PATTERNS

Design patterns provide low-level, reusable solutions to common problems related to implementation. In other words, they provide implementation details to solve specific design problems in code.

On the other hand, design patterns are specific to a programming language that produces implementation details and they enhance code maintainability and scalability. Singleton pattern, factory pattern, adapter, and MVC pattern are an example of these design patterns.

DESIGN PRINCPLES

Design principles provide high-level and best practices guidelines on making it easier to design. They are the most essential concepts for software to be maintainable and flexible.

Unlike design patterns, design principles cannot be limited to any specific programming language. Ideally, developers should think about design principles at the top of the implementation process. SOLID (Single Responsibility, Open-Closed, Liskov Substitution, Interface Segregation, and Dependency Injection), DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself), KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid), and YAGNI (You Aint' Gonna Need It) are some of the most well-known design principles.

CONCLUSION

Two concepts improve the code quality and offer maintainable, flexible, and reusable software products. While design patterns provide low-level solutions to specific design problems, design principles represent guidelines for making it easier to design.

By distinguishing between their differences in design patterns and design principles, software products can be developed with higher quality, maintainability, flexibility, and reusability. This understanding allows developers to choose the appropriate approach for each situation and create more efficient and effective solutions.
